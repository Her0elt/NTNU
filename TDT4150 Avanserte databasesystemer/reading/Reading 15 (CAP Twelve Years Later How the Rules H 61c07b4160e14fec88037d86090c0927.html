The CAP theorem states that a networked shared-data system can only have two out of three desirable properties: consistency, availability, and tolerance to network partitions. This has led to the development of a wide range of new distributed systems in the past decade, as well as debate on the relative merits of consistency and availability. However, the "2 of 3" formulation is misleading and oversimplifies the tensions among properties, and designers must consider nuances when making trade-offs. While designers still need to choose between consistency and availability when partitions are present, there is a great deal of flexibility for handling partitions and recovering from them. The modern CAP goal is to maximize combinations of consistency and availability that make sense for the specific application, incorporating plans for operation during a partition and recovery afterward.
